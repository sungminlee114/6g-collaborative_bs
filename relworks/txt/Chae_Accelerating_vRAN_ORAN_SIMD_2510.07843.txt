Abstract—The evolution of radio access networks (RANs)
toward virtualization and openness creates new opportunities
for flexible, cost-effective, and high-performance deployments.
Achieving real-time and energy-efficient baseband processing on
commercial off-the-shelf platforms, however, remains a critical
challenge. This article explores how single instruction multiple
data (SIMD) architectures can accelerate RAN workloads. We
first outline why key physical-layer functions, such as channel
estimation, multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) detection,
and forward error correction, are well aligned with SIMD’s
data-level parallelism. We then present practical design guide-
lines and prototype results, showing significant improvements
in throughput and energy efficiency compared to conventional
CPU-only processing, while retaining programmability and ease
of integration. Finally, we discuss open challenges in workload
balancing and hardware heterogeneity, and highlight the role
of SIMD as an enabling technology for flexible, efficient, and
sustainable 6G-ready RANs.
I. INTRODUCTION
T
HE architecture of the radio access network (RAN) is
continuing to evolve in the transition from 4G to 5G
and beyond to 6G. This evolution is driven by several key
factors. First, RANs must meet some performance require-
ments, including support for higher data rates, lower latency,
and operation across a wide range of frequency bands. Second,
there is a desire to get more vendors to open RAN archi-
tectures, enabling multi-vendor interoperability and reducing
dependence on proprietary hardware. Third, the emergence
of new deployment models such as cloud RAN and edge
computing necessitates more flexible and software-centric
RAN architectures. These trends are motivating a shift away
from hardware-defined solutions toward more modular and
virtualized designs that can adapt to future requirements with
greater agility.
The traditional RAN (tRAN) architecture has been widely
deployed in 4G and 5G. In a tRAN system, most of the
baseband processing is performed on dedicated hardware such
as field programmable gate arrays (FPGAs), digital signal
processors (DSPs), and application-specific integrated circuits
(ASICs). These platforms offer low latency and some level
of programmability, which made them attractive for initial

alternative architectures that are easier to maintain.
Virtualized RAN (vRAN) is one alternative architecture. In
vRAN, the baseband functions are implemented in software
and run on commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) servers instead

vRAN also supports more flexible deployment models, such
as centralized or cloud-based processing. The open RAN (O-
RAN) framework builds on the idea of vRAN by defining

This allows components from different vendors to work to-
gether, enabling multi-vendor interoperability and reducing
dependence on proprietary solutions. As shown in Fig. 1,
vRAN and O-RAN architectures together promote a more
open and modular approach to RAN design, lowering the
barrier for competition.
A key feature of vRAN is that it runs mainly on general-
purpose processors (GPPs), typically x86-64 processors, in-
stead of dedicated baseband hardware. These CPUs are flexible
and widely available, allowing operators to implement RAN
more easily and reduce deployment complexity. While GPUs
are explored for specific use cases, their higher power con-
sumption and integration challenges have limited mainstream
adoption. To fully benefit from CPU-based processing, the
software must be optimized to take advantage of the CPU’s
architecture. One important technique is single instruction,
multiple data (SIMD), which has been a hot topic in modern
vRAN and O-RAN baseband implementations. SIMD allows
the same operation to be applied to multiple data elements
in parallel. It has been widely used in fields like computer
vision and image processing, where parallel operations on
thousands of pixels are required in real-time tasks such as au-
tonomous vehicles and robots [2]. In wireless systems, SIMD
can be applied to core baseband functions such as multiple-
input multiple-output (MIMO) detection, which involves large
vector and matrix computations [3]. This approach enables
faster physical (PHY) layer processing without relying on
external accelerators. Recent research has also addressed how
to optimize data arrangement and memory access patterns in

in modern RAN architectures. While SIMD is one of the
features in general-purpose CPUs and widely used in domains
like computer architecture and image processing, it is not

of software in baseband processing. Then, we explain the
fundamentals of SIMD and why it is well suited for the

A. The vRAN architecture
vRAN refers to the software-defined implementation of
RAN functions that enables baseband functions, or at least
some of them, to run on GPPs, such as x86-64 CPUs, virtually
on top of COTS computing platforms. The vRAN architecture
separates software from hardware, enabling RAN workloads to
operate on GPP-based servers. Virtualization on x86-64 plat-
forms allows a unified software stack to support a wide range
of deployment scenarios from small cells to macro cell base
stations. Industry developers can implement RAN functions
using their familiar programming languages such as C or C++,
and port them across processors with minimal effort through
recompilation. In contrast, tRAN architectures perform PHY
processing on hardwired fixed-function silicon, which requires
hardware upgrades for new services. Legacy systems also
depend on hard-coded hardware description languages (HDLs)
such as Verilog. These are specialized programming languages
used to describe digital circuit behavior at the register transfer
level (RTL). This approach ties the design to specific hardware
and require proprietary software tools, limiting innovation due
to the need for developers with specialized expertise.
The evolution of vRAN architecture from virtualized net-
work functions (VNFs) to cloud-native or containerized net-
work functions (CNFs) significantly enhances operational ef-
ficiency. As illustrated in Fig. 2, VNFs run within virtual
machines (VMs) and require full guest operating systems
(OSs) over a hypervisor, a software layer that creates and
manages multiple VMs on a physical COTS server. While
VNFs provided the first step toward virtualization, they are
resource-heavy and slower to scale. CNFs, in contrast, operate
as lightweight containers generated by a container engine. This
containerized structure eliminates the need for a full guest

B. The O-RAN architecture
O-RAN extends the principles of vRAN by introducing
open standard interfaces and enabling interoperability among
equipment from different vendors [6]. Although commercial
adoption is still limited compared to tRAN and vRAN, O-
RAN’s main motivation is cost reduction through disaggrega-
tion of PHY-layer functions and increased vendor competition.
Open interfaces also allow local vendors to enter the RAN
ecosystem, promoting diversity and strengthening technology
sovereignty. In the long term, O-RAN adoption is expected to
help maintain 5G leadership and influence the development of
6G systems.
At the functional level, O-RAN separates legacy RAN
modules into the radio unit (RU), distributed unit (DU), and
centralized unit (CU), connected by standardized interfaces
such as fronthaul and midhaul. This functional split allows in-
dependent development and deployment of each unit, increas-
ing flexibility in vendor selection and network design. Among
the various functional split options, Option 7 represents a
balanced trade-off between fronthaul bandwidth requirements
and RU complexity. Reducing the dimensionality of signals
sent over the fronthaul is also an active research topic [7], as
it can significantly lower the processing load between the RU
and DU.
In O-RAN deployments, the pursuit of both energy effi-
ciency and low latency aligns closely with the capabilities of
SIMD-based acceleration. As 6G moves toward higher perfor-
mance, greater flexibility, and improved sustainability, split-
aware functional optimization will be essential for reducing
baseband energy consumption [8]. At the same time, achieving
real-time performance requires lightweight and accelerated
implementations of the DU and RU. Maximizing the use of
internal processing resources becomes critical as 6G faces
strict latency requirements and processes much larger data.
In this context, SIMD-based acceleration enables vRAN and
O-RAN platforms to meet high throughput and real-time
processing demands. It also supports the energy efficiency and
latency goals of next-generation RAN systems.
III. SIMD FUNDAMENTALS AND INTEGRATION INTO PHY
A. SIMD fundamentals
SIMD is a parallel computing architecture that enables a
single instruction to operate on multiple data elements. It
was originally introduced by Intel through extensions such as
MMX, SSE, and AVX, and is now widely supported across
modern CPU architectures. Compared to non-SIMD execution,
SIMD enables fine-grained data-level parallelism directly on
each CPU core and can achieve speedups of 8x to 16x for
signal processing tasks.
SIMD acceleration is realized by defining specialized data
types (e.g., __m256 and __m128 for Intel AVX) and ap-
plying vectorized arithmetic instructions such as add, subs,
mul, and div. Fig. 3(a) demonstrates this concept using a
simple element-wise array addition example. The left side
of the figure shows a standard scalar loop, which processes
one element at a time. The right figure shows SIMD in-
trinsics (e.g., __m256_add_pd for double precision and
__m256_add_ps for single precision) that process multiple
elements in parallel within a single CPU instruction like four
packed-double (PD) or eight packed-single (PS) values in
a 256-bit register. This example highlights how SIMD ex-
ploits data-level parallelism to accelerate computation. A key
practical advantage of SIMD intrinsics is their compatibility
with standard C/C++ environments, including compilers (e.g.,
GCC) and debuggers (e.g., GDB). This compatibility simpli-
fies development and debugging across generations of Intel
CPUs and similar architectures. Provided as C function-like
application programming interfaces (APIs), SIMD intrinsics
directly map to specific SIMD assembly instructions and offer
much better readability than raw assembly. These features
make SIMD a portable and sustainable option for PHY-layer
processing.
The more efficiently communication engineers optimize an
algorithm for SIMD, the greater the acceleration gains they
can achieve. Modern CPUs support extended SIMD instruction
sets such as Intel AVX2 and AVX-512, which provide 256-
bit and 512-bit vector registers, respectively. These extensions
enable high-throughput floating-point operations (FLOPs), es-
sential for computationally intensive MIMO workloads in-
volving dense matrix and vector algebra. Fully leveraging
SIMD requires careful memory alignment, loop unrolling, and

B.
SIMD integration into PHY
SIMD shows its benefits when applied to heavy PHY opera-
tions in modern RAN systems. In the context of vRAN and O-
RAN, SIMD can accelerate a wide range of PHY-layer blocks,
including MIMO detection, precoding and channel estimation.
Among these, MIMO detection is chosen as the primary focus
of this article because it is computationally demanding yet
algorithmically structured, making it an ideal candidate for
SIMD-based acceleration. As shown in Fig. 3(b), the fused
multiply-add (FMA) example maps directly to intermediate
operations in MIMO receivers such as covariance calculation.
These repetitive linear algebraic tasks benefit significantly
from SIMD’s data-level parallelism through vectorized addi-
tive and multiplicative instructions. We use MIMO detection as
a case study to demonstrate how SIMD can be systematically
integrated into broader PHY-layer pipelines in vRAN and
O-RAN systems. Offloading these tasks to SIMD-enabled
software modules allows vRAN systems to achieve lower
latency and higher throughput without the need for GPUs or
FPGAs.
SIMD-based baseband processing also runs directly on CPU
cores, minimizing PCIe-related data transfer overhead and
enabling tight timing control. These benefits are especially
valuable in commercial vRAN and O-RAN systems that rely
on fully software-defined signal chains running on COTS
servers. Intel’s recent white paper demonstrates the viability
of this approach by implementing a SIMD-accelerated polar
decoder using AVX-512 for 5G control channels, showing that
even latency-sensitive L1 modules can be handled entirely in
software on x86-64 platforms [9]. This prior work targets a
simple decoding task with low computational complexity and
small payloads, such as control channels.

Fig. 4 depicts the detailed structure of linear minimum
mean square error (LMMSE) MIMO detection algorithms.
It highlights that matrix inversion remains one of the most
computationally demanding tasks. To reduce this burden, we
implement LU decomposition, which factorizes matrices into
lower (L) and upper (U) triangular components [10]. This
transformation simplifies the inversion problem into two se-
quential steps: forward substitution and backward substitution,
consisting of simple arithmetic operations that are highly
suitable for SIMD vectorization.
In the simulation, we configure LDPC channel encod-
ing/decoding and MIMO-OFDM transmission using 15 kHz
subcarrier spacing and 60 resource blocks (RB), corresponding
to 720 subcarriers. We use modulation and coding scheme
(MCS) index 9, as specified in [11], to measure the average
processing time per TTI for MIMO detection over 10,000
TTIs. The channel model is based on the 3GPP TDL-C
specification. We evaluate signal detection performance using
a LMMSE receiver, which involves inverting the received
signal covariance matrix, R. Fig. 5(a) illustrates that SIMD PS
instructions achieve detection accuracy nearly identical to that
of PD, despite the reduced arithmetic precision. This obser-
vation confirms that in practical MIMO scenarios, significant
acceleration can be obtained without compromising detection
performance. As the dimension of R increases with antenna
scaling, we assess the computational efficiency of SIMD
optimization in handling the resulting matrix operations. The
evaluation was conducted on an Intel Core i9-14900KF CPU
with AVX2 support. This configuration enables 256-bit SIMD
operations using intrinsic data types such as __m256. These
instructions allow parallel processing of multiple complex
numbers per clock cycle, up to two 64-bit complex numbers
(with 32-bit real and imaginary parts each) or four 32-bit
complex numbers (with 16-bit real and imaginary parts each).
Compared to a baseline vRAN configuration without SIMD
acceleration, SIMD-based MIMO processing demonstrates up
to 50% speedup, primarily due to optimization of the matrix
inversion stage. As shown in Fig. 5(b), this performance
gain is consistently observed across different MIMO sizes
(2 × 2, 4 × 4, 8 × 8), where matrix inversion accounts for
the largest portion of processing time and benefits the most
from SIMD acceleration. Although this step is the primary
computational bottleneck in linear MIMO receivers, SIMD
offers the greatest opportunity for reducing end-to-end latency.
When reducing data precision from double to single (float),
the system maintains comparable accuracy while achieving
additional acceleration gains. As vRAN manufacturers in-
creasingly adopt high-performance CPUs, using AVX-512
instructions with 512-bit vector registers can further maximize
the processing speed. This trend highlights the potential of
lightweight, software-native techniques for meeting real-time
PHY processing deadlines in vRAN deployments.
We also analyze the maximum data rates of PHY-layer and
evaluate the scalability of SIMD acceleration under different
MIMO configurations. To estimate the theoretical peak rates,
we consider a 5G NR system operating with maximum MCS
level(27), which uses 256-QAM modulation and a high coding
rate. Leveraging the MCS, RB allocation, and the number of
data streams, we can calculate the total PHY-layer payload
bits over 1 TTI, known as transport block size (TBS) [11].
With a 60 RB allocation and 15 kHz subcarrier spacing,
each 2 × 2 MIMO carries approximately 139,376 bits per slot
(or 139.4 kbit over 1 ms), resulting in a peak physical-layer
throughput of 139.4 Mbps. Extending this to a 4×4 MIMO
configuration yields about 278,776 bits per slot, or 279 Mbps.
These values represent realistic PHY-layer payloads under 1
ms TTI constraints.
Targeting 4 × 4 MIMO for indoor deployments, the above
configuration is appropriate, and our vRAN prototype demon-
strates that SIMD acceleration reduces PHY processing time
by more than 50 % compared to a scalar implementation. This
enables one of the most demanding tasks, such as 4×4 MIMO
detection, to be completed within 0.03 ms. The processing
time accounts for only about 3% of the 1 ms TTI, leaving a
substantial margin for other baseband operations. Such perfor-
mance confirms that all PHY computations can be completed
within the 1 TTI deadline. The evaluation results validate that
software-defined base stations can meet real-time constraints

bandwidths (i.e., more RBs) are achievable on commercial
high-performance CPUs with AVX-512 support. Wider SIMD
lanes and efficient vector load/store operations increase the
data rates that can be processed in a single TTI while still
meeting latency requirements. This capability supports the re-
alization of fully software-defined indoor vRAN deployments
with Gbps-level throughput.
V. HIGH-LEVEL DESIGN TRADE-OFFS
In vRAN systems, several acceleration options exist for
PHY-layer processing, including CPU-based SIMD, GPU-
based single instruction, multiple threads (SIMT), and FPGA
solutions. Among these, SIMD operates directly on general-
purpose CPUs and is well-suited for latency-sensitive work-
loads. It also executes vectorized instructions on CPU registers
with minimal control overhead and data transfer latency.
Table I demonstrates the strengths and weaknesses of GPUs
in vRAN PHY acceleration. GPUs offer massive thread-
level parallelism and can achieve very high throughput. In
signal processing tasks, however, they often require substantial
software tuning to reach peak performance. SIMT execution
also faces warp divergence, where threads within a warp take
different control paths, and scheduling overhead. These factors
introduce latency variability and reduce energy efficiency.
They also make GPUs less suitable for latency-critical real-
time workloads.
FPGA-based solutions provide deterministic latency and
excellent energy efficiency. They also deliver high timing
precision, which is essential for time-critical PHY-layer tasks.
These benefits come with trade-offs, however, as they require
long development cycles, reliance on proprietary toolchains,
and limited flexibility after deployment. Such constraints re-
duce agility and slow adaptation to evolving RAN require-
ments, particularly in software-defined environments.
SIMD applies uniform vector operations across aligned data
vectors. This approach eliminates the per-thread divergence
and unpredictable delays seen in GPUs. Its software-oriented
design removes the need for device passthroughs or vendor-
specific drivers and allows seamless integration within CNF-
based vRAN deployments. Overall, SIMD presents a bal-
anced combination of scalability, low integration overhead,
and compatibility with standard x86-64 server platforms. This
balance makes it attractive for near-term 5G and emerging
6G RAN deployments, where ease of deployment, real-time
performance, and software sustainability are crucial.
VI. FUTURE OUTLOOK
SIMD-based acceleration is a practical approach for scal-
able and efficient MIMO processing in vRAN and O-RAN
deployments. In this article, an NR-compliant, end-to-end
link-level simulator on a COTS platform was used to em-
ulate commercial base station operations. Results show that
SIMD can significantly improve the performance of key PHY-
layer functions such as matrix inversion and signal detection.
Modern CPUs equipped with advanced SIMD instruction sets
(e.g., AVX2 and AVX-512) provide substantial acceleration,
reinforcing SIMD’s role as an practical enabler in software-
defined RAN environments. Beyond demonstrating technical
feasibility, it is essential to understand how SIMD can be
applied in diverse use cases and what future research directions
can extend its impact.
A. Use cases and future research directions
The application of SIMD goes beyond PHY-layer testing
and extends to diverse deployment scenarios. Several repre-
sentative examples include:
• Distributed MIMO (D-MIMO): In D-MIMO [12], spa-
tially distributed RUs cooperate to serve users, creating
high fronthaul traffic and a heavy computational load on
the DU. SIMD can accelerate both fronthaul compres-
sion using block floating-point (BFP) quantization and
centralized baseband processing. These support real-time
coordination across DUs and enable efficient, scalable D-
MIMO operation.
• Green 6G RAN: As 6G targets sustainability and car-
bon neutrality, energy efficiency in RAN architectures
is a critical concern [13], [14]. SIMD-based processing
addresses this by executing PHY-layer tasks efficiently
on general-purpose CPUs. It reduces data movement and
avoids the overhead of device passthrough. Its determinis-
tic execution and tight software integration make it well-
suited for energy-aware baseband pipelines in Green RAN
deployments.
• Indoor fixed wireless access (FWA): FWA is a cost-
effective broadband alternative to fiber and delivers high-
speed internet to fixed locations without the need for
physical cabling [15]. It is particularly valuable in ru-
ral areas, helping bridge the internet access gap. It is
also well-suited for enterprise or industrial sites that
7
require rapid, flexible deployments. In O-RAN deploy-
ments running on COTS servers, FWA’s high bandwidth
and predictable traffic patterns create heavy PHY-layer
processing demands. SIMD acceleration can handle these
workload efficiently.
• Artificial intelligence (AI) RAN: While AI/ML work-
loads typically run on GPUs or tensor processing units
(TPUs), low-level PHY tasks can be offloaded to SIMD.
This frees the accelerators to focus on control-plane
functions within RAN intelligent controllers (RICs), in-
cluding resource scheduling, energy-efficient operation,
and quality of experience (QoE) optimization. As AI
RAN architectures evolve toward 6G, this funtional split
between SIMD and AI accelerators is expected to become
important for achieving both real-time performance and
system stability.
B. The road ahead: hybrid and heterogeneous computing
Hybrid and heterogeneous computing techniques will play
a critical role in future RAN architectures, combining (1)
SIMD for deterministic, fine-grained PHY acceleration, (2)
GPUs/TPUs for AI control plane processing, and (3) FP-
GAs and photonic accelerators for ultra-low-latency or high-
throughput tasks supporting macro cells. Particularly, consider-
ing the characteristics of optical fiber and related components
in O-RAN fronthaul, photonic accelerators can offload many
baseband processing blocks. Their ultrafast, power-efficient
capabilities complement electronic SIMD and FPGA solutions.
In this broader vision, SIMD is not only an optimization but
a cornerstone of scalable, agile, and maintainable PHY-layer
processing. Its inherent compatibility with COTS servers and
software stacks provides a pathway to realizing open, flexible,
and high-performance RANs for 5G-Advanced and beyond.
We hope this work encourages deeper exploration of SIMD-
based acceleration strategies within the wireless research and
communication engineering communities.
